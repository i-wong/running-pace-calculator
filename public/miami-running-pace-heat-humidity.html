Miami Marathon & Running Pace Adjustment — Heat & Humidity

Miami is one of the toughest environments for distance running on the planet. Year-round heat and oppressive humidity mean that nearly any race pace you've set in cooler conditions will need to be relaxed. The Miami Marathon in late January or February is the "cool season" — and it still regularly sees race-day temps above 75 °F with 80%+ humidity.

Key Facts

  • Miami's average high in January (marathon month) is 77 °F (25 °C); humidity regularly exceeds 80%.
  • At 82 °F and 85% humidity, the heat index approaches 90 °F — serious stress for runners running for 2+ hours.
  • The city is essentially at sea level, so altitude is not a factor; heat and humidity are the entire story.
  • Sweat evaporation — the body's primary cooling mechanism — is severely impaired when humidity is above 75%.

Race Day Tips

  • Start at least 60–90 seconds per mile slower than your goal pace and reassess at mile 6.
  • Wear light, moisture-wicking clothing; avoid cotton entirely.
  • Use every water stop. Pouring water on your head and wrists provides meaningful cooling.
  • If the heat index exceeds 90 °F, treat any finish as a success and do not chase a time goal.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much slower should I run in Miami vs a cooler city?

A runner trained in 55 °F / 60% humidity racing in 82 °F / 85% humidity can expect a 10–15% slower pace — that's 1–2 minutes per mile for a recreational runner. Enter your goal pace in the calculator above with the Miami preset for a precise estimate.

Is the Miami Marathon a good race for a PR?

Rarely. The heat and humidity almost always make Miami significantly slower than races in cooler climates. It's a great experience race but most runners plan their PR attempts for fall or spring races in cooler regions.
