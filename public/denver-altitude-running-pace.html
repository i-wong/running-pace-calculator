Running Pace at Altitude — Denver & Mile High Adjustment

Denver sits at exactly 5,280 feet (1,609 m) above sea level — the "Mile High City." At that altitude, the air contains roughly 17% less oxygen than at sea level. Runners who live and train near sea level typically run 3–5% slower at Denver's elevation, with the effect most pronounced in longer aerobic efforts like half marathons and marathons.

Key Facts

  • At 5,280 ft there is about 17% less oxygen per breath than at sea level.
  • VO2max drops roughly 1.5–3% per 1,000 feet above ~3,000 ft.
  • Altitude effects are most pronounced in paces that rely heavily on aerobic capacity (half marathon, marathon) and least in short sprint efforts.
  • Acclimatization begins within 24–48 hours but full aerobic adaptation takes 2–4 weeks.
  • Dehydration risk is higher at altitude — dry Colorado air increases respiratory moisture loss.

Race Day Tips

  • Arrive at least 3 days before your race OR arrive within 24 hours and race before full altitude effects set in (the first ~12 hours you still have your sea-level blood composition).
  • Slow your easy and long runs by 30–60 seconds per mile during the first week at altitude.
  • Drink an extra 500–750 ml of water per day to compensate for increased respiratory fluid loss.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much slower will I run in Denver vs sea level?

A sea-level runner typically runs 3–5% slower at 5,280 ft for distances of 5K and longer. For an 8:00/mi runner that is roughly 15–25 seconds per mile. Use the calculator above with altitude 5280 ft and your goal pace for a precise estimate.

Does altitude affect shorter races like 5K?

Yes, but less than longer races. The aerobic cost is the same; it just matters more when you're racing for 30+ minutes. A 5K racer might lose 2–3%; a marathoner might lose 4–6% or more if not acclimatized.

Should I adjust my marathon pace for the Colfax or Revel Canyon City race?

Yes — both are run at or above Denver's elevation. Use this calculator with the race altitude and forecast temperature to get a realistic goal pace before race day.
